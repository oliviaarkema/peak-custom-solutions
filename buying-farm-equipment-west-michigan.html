A Buyer's Checklist for West Michigan Equipment Purchases

Buying Used Farm Equipment in West Michigan

Buying used farm equipment is a significant investment. This checklist covers what to inspect, what to ask, and what to think through before you commit — so you can buy with confidence.

Before You Start

Know What You Need Before You Shop

The West Michigan farm equipment market has active buyers and sellers across a wide range of equipment categories. Good machines move quickly, and having a clear picture of what you need — horsepower, configuration, hours, and budget — before you start looking saves time and reduces the risk of a hasty decision.

Think through how the equipment will be used, what compatibility requirements exist with your current fleet or attachments, and whether financing will be part of the purchase. Getting those answers sorted upfront puts you in a much stronger position when the right piece comes available.

The Checklist

What to Check Before You Buy

Whether you're buying a tractor, combine, sprayer, or any other piece of farm equipment, these are the fundamentals to cover.

Hours & Usage

Verify hours on the meter and ask how the equipment was used — field conditions, crop types, and seasonal workloads all affect wear beyond what hours alone indicate.

Maintenance Records

Ask for any available service records. Consistent maintenance history is a strong indicator of how the machine was cared for. Missing records aren't always a red flag, but they do add risk.

Known Issues & Repairs

Ask the seller directly about any known problems, recent repairs, or recurring issues. A straightforward seller will tell you. Hesitation or vague answers on this question are worth noting.

Visual Inspection

Look for leaks at hydraulic connections, cylinders, and around the engine. Check tires or tracks for wear. Look at wear points on attachments, headers, and moving parts.

Operational Test

Start and run the equipment if at all possible. Listen for unusual noises, check for warning lights, test hydraulics, and engage any major systems before agreeing to buy.

Title & Ownership

Confirm the seller has clear title and the ability to transfer ownership. Verify that serial numbers on the machine match any documentation provided.

Do Your Homework

Questions to Ask the Seller

Why Are You Selling?

The answer often tells you something useful — upgrading, downsizing, retiring, or transitioning operations are all legitimate reasons. Vague or evasive answers deserve follow-up.

Has It Had Any Major Repairs?

Major engine, transmission, or structural repairs aren't automatically disqualifying — but knowing about them lets you price accordingly and understand the machine's history.

Is Financing Available?

If you plan to finance the purchase, knowing whether the seller works with financing partners — or whether you need to arrange your own — affects your timeline and negotiating position.

Don't Overlook the Details

Financing and Transportation

Two things buyers often underestimate: the cost and complexity of moving large equipment, and how long financing approval can take.

If the equipment is being transported, get a clear understanding of who is responsible for arranging and paying for trucking before you finalize the deal. Equipment transport costs vary significantly by distance and equipment size.

If you're financing, start the process early. Having pre-approval or a financing relationship in place before you find the right piece means you can move quickly when it matters.
